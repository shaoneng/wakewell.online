What is a Sleep Cycle?

Sleep isn't a monolithic state. Instead, we cycle through different stages of sleep throughout the night. A single, complete sleep cycle lasts approximately 90 minutes and consists of two main types of sleep:

  • Non-REM (NREM) Sleep: This is broken down into three stages, moving from light sleep to deep, restorative sleep. This is when your body repairs tissues, builds bone and muscle, and strengthens the immune system.
  • REM (Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep: This is the stage most associated with dreaming. Your brain is highly active, and this phase is vital for memory consolidation, learning, and mood regulation.

The Problem with Waking Up Mid-Cycle

The groggy, disoriented feeling you experience when waking up is known as sleep inertia. This feeling is significantly more intense when your alarm jolts you out of a deep NREM sleep stage. However, at the end of a 90-minute cycle, you are naturally in a much lighter stage of sleep, making it far easier to wake up feeling alert and ready to start your day.

This is the principle our Sleep Calculator is built upon. By planning to wake up at the end of a 4th, 5th, or 6th cycle, you align your wake-up time with your body's natural rhythm.

How to Apply the 90-Minute Rule

  1. Decide Your Wake-Up Time: Start with the time you need to be up.
  2. Count Backwards in 90-Minute Increments: Most adults need 5-6 cycles, which translates to 7.5 to 9 hours of sleep.
    • 6 cycles = 9 hours
    • 5 cycles = 7.5 hours
    • 4 cycles = 6 hours
  3. Factor in Sleep Onset: Remember to add about 15 minutes for the time it takes to actually fall asleep. For instance, if you want to wake up at 7:00 AM after 5 cycles (7.5 hours), you should aim to be in bed by 11:15 PM.

By using this simple method, you can stop fighting your alarm clock and start waking up on the right side of the bed, every single day.
